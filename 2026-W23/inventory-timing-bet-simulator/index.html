A-items and predictable X demand receive the strongest service protection and cost tolerance. Lower-value or volatile segments carry tighter tolerance, so the same tariff, freight, or supplier shock can produce a different recommendation across the portfolio.

Operator guide

How to run and read the simulation

Start with one SKU family, make the inputs approximate your planning conversation, then switch scenarios to see which positions remain service buffers and which become timing risks.

1

Set the segment

ABC is value or commercial importance. XYZ is demand predictability. AX gets the strongest service promise; CZ gets the tightest cash discipline.

2

Edit the operating signals

Days and value show the size of the bet. Demand, tariff, freight, supplier, and capital are 0-100 pressure scores: higher means the signal is more important to the decision.

3

Read the policy chip

The chip under each SKU shows the service target and cost tolerance implied by ABC/XYZ. High tolerance means the model allows more cost to protect availability.

4

Compare baseline to scenario

The row result shows baseline risk into simulated risk. A rising number means the scenario makes the position more exposed; a falling number means the scenario relieves pressure.

5

Use the recommendation

Hold means demand or supplier risk justifies the buffer. Reclassify means the hedge may be rational but should not be managed like service stock. Stop-buy means cash and aging are the priority.

6

Check the portfolio read

The right panel totals value, high-risk dollars, average risk shift, exception count, and reason mix so the meeting can focus on the exposed tail instead of debating every pallet.

Scenario meanings

  • Soft landing: demand holds while freight, tariff, supplier, and cash pressure ease.
  • Bullwhip hangover: demand fades, days rise, and working-capital pressure gets worse.
  • Policy and freight squeeze: tariff, freight, and supplier pressure all increase together.

Reason codes

  • Service Buffer: inventory exists to protect availability.
  • Tariff/Freight Hedge: inventory exists because a cost clock moved the buy earlier.
  • Supplier-Risk Buffer: inventory exists because lead times or reliability are unstable.
  • Old Forecast Risk: inventory may be solving yesterday's demand assumption.
