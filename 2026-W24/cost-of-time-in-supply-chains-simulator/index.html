Decision logic

The cheapest lane is not always the cheapest calendar.

Each row scores the cost of waiting, the cost of moving early, and the cost of being wrong. A high score does not always mean ship now; it means the order needs an explicit clock owner and a threshold-triggered action.

Operator guide

How to run and read the simulation

Use one category, lane, supplier program, or PO wave at a time. The goal is not to forecast the exact future. The goal is to decide which timing risk gets paid for, delayed, split, or stopped.

1

Define the order family

Replace sample rows with SKU families or open POs. Use value at landed cost so margin and cash effects are visible.

2

Score the clocks

Rate freight/fuel, tariff, compliance, demand, and supplier pressure from 0 to 100. Higher means the clock matters more.

3

Set deadline days

Use days until a tariff, surcharge, booking cutoff, delivery promise, or supplier documentation deadline. Short windows raise late-decision risk.

4

Check margin buffer

Low margin makes a paid timing premium harder to absorb. High service criticality can still justify it.

5

Compare scenarios

The row shows baseline risk into scenario risk. A large positive delta means the selected future makes that PO more fragile.

6

Use the action

Actions are meeting prompts: book now, split, wait with trigger, hold for documents, re-source, reprice, or stop the buy.

Scenario meanings

  • The premium works: early bookings protect service and demand absorbs the goods.
  • Air pocket arrives: June pull-forward borrowed from July/August and inventory ages.
  • Policy and fuel squeeze: tariff details stay uncertain, fuel stays high, and documentation gaps become expensive.

Primary clock codes

  • Carrier clock: spot rates, GRIs, fuel pass-through, capacity and blank sailings.
  • Policy clock: tariff rates, USTR process dates, country exposure and exemptions.
  • Compliance clock: origin evidence, forced-labor documentation and broker readiness.
  • Retail clock: demand proof, promotion dates, seasonal promise and sell-through risk.
  • Finance clock: margin buffer, working capital and repricing window.
